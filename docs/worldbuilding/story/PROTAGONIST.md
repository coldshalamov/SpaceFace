**SPACEFACE — THE PROTAGONIST: WREN**

---

**Who he is now:**

Wren flies a ship called the Tessera that someone else stole and someone else abandoned and someone else gave him because they owed him a debt that couldn't be paid any other way. He is a hacker who learned to pilot because hacking requires a ship and piloting requires not dying. He is not good at violence. He is good at not being where violence happens, which is a different skill that looks the same from the outside.

He talks like a card dealer: steady, precise, slightly too fast for you to catch the tell. When he's comfortable he talks too much. When he's working he says nothing. There is no middle register. People mistake the talking for friendliness and the silence for hostility. Both are wrong. The talking is how he reads the room. The silence is how he survives it.

He is thirty-one years old. He has been in this part of the galaxy for nineteen years. He does not know the name of this part of the galaxy in any language that was spoken where he grew up. He knows the trade routes, the gate sequences, the dock codes, the price of reactor fuel at Tycho on a Tuesday, the way Kessler's thumb sits on the scale when the load is heavy. He does not know how to get home. He stopped trying to find out eleven years ago. He started again six months ago, when a job went wrong and something he hadn't seen since he was twelve years old was sitting in the cargo hold wrapped in packing foam and mislabeled as industrial composite.

---

**Where he's from:**

A planet that doesn't appear on any chart in this part of the galaxy. Not classified, not redacted — simply not indexed. Humanity spread far enough that the places it started from became trivia, then rumor, then nothing. His planet was an agricultural world with one city and a university and weather that made people complain in the way people complain about weather when nothing worse has happened yet. He was twelve when something worse happened.

They came in a ship that blotted the sun. Not a metaphor — the ship was large enough that its shadow crossed the horizon and the temperature dropped three degrees in the time it took to look up. The ship did not communicate. The ship did not demand surrender. The ship opened and things came out of it.

---

**The Vethari:**

They were not always what they are now.

The species that humans would later call the Vethari — a transliteration of the sound they make when they speak their own name, which is a sound that involves throat structures humans don't have — were, by every account that predates the corruption, a civilization built on obligation. They recorded debts the way humans record history. Every exchange was logged, every favor owed, every insult catalogued. Their architecture was ledger-work in stone. Their music was the recitation of what was owed and by whom. They were not warriors in the way that word implies — they were accountants of violence, precise and transactional. A wrong was answered with an equal wrong, measured to the gram. They called this balance. It was the only moral framework they had and it worked for longer than human civilization has existed.

The fungus changed that.

Nobody knows where it came from. Not the Vethari, not the few human xenobiologists who've studied recovered tissue samples in classified labs on planets Wren has never heard of. The fungus is a filamentary organism — whitish-reddish threads, fine as cobweb, that colonizes the Vethari central nervous system through the respiratory tract. It grows slowly. It connects. The filaments bridge the gap between individual neural architectures, creating a shared perceptual field — what a human neurologist would call involuntary telepathy. The Vethari would call it the Ledger made flesh, if they could still call it anything.

The problem is that the connection doesn't discriminate. Every sensation, every memory, every impulse crosses the bridge. A Vethari who feels rage transmits rage to every connected individual within range. A Vethari who feels pain transmits pain. A Vethari who feels grief — and they grieve constantly, for what they were — transmits grief that arrives in the minds of creatures already drowning in transmitted rage and transmitted pain, and the grief becomes fuel for more rage because the neural architecture can no longer distinguish between the emotion and the response.

The result is not an army. It is a species-wide psychotic break that has been ongoing for longer than anyone can date. Individual Vethari retain consciousness. They know what they are doing. They cannot stop doing it because stopping requires a moment of neurological isolation that the fungus will not permit. The filaments maintain the connection even during sleep. Especially during sleep.

What they do to planets is not conquest. Conquest implies intent. What the Vethari do is what happens when a hundred thousand minds share every killing impulse simultaneously and the architecture that once measured violence to the gram can no longer measure anything at all. They don't occupy. They swarm, they break, they move on. The ship stays in orbit. The things that come out of it do what the fungus makes them do. When the planet is used up — when there is nothing left to trigger the rage-loss-rage cycle — the ship moves. The Vethari inside return to something that looks like catatonia until the next world.

Between the violence, in the ship, they sit with images of what they were. Wren has seen this. They hold objects — small things, carvings, data wafers — and the sound they make is the sound of their own name, repeated, which is also the word for what is owed, which is also the word for what was lost. The sound does not stop. The fungus transmits it.

---

**What happened to Wren:**

He was twelve. He was good with machines, which on an agricultural world meant he fixed irrigation controllers and complained about the firmware. He had a sister named Lida who was nine and who drew pictures of the university buildings she planned to attend when she was old enough, and who had never in her life seen anything that wasn't grass or rain or the single city's skyline from the east road.

When the shadow crossed the horizon, Wren's mother told him to take Lida to the crawlspace beneath the grain silo at the edge of the family's land. The crawlspace was a maintenance access point for the silo's climate control system. It was designed for one adult technician. It fit two children if neither of them moved. The grain silo above them was full. The hatch was hidden by the silo's foundation cladding. His mother said she would come back. She did not come back.

Wren brought Lida food and water from the house for eleven days. On the twelfth day the house was not there. On the thirteenth day he was caught in the open by three Vethari who were taller than the irrigation towers and who smelled like copper and something sweet that he later identified as the fungus's metabolic byproduct. They took him to the ship. They did not take him to the crawlspace because they did not know about the crawlspace.

He does not know if Lida is alive. The grain in the silo would have lasted months if she rationed. The climate control would have kept the crawlspace habitable. The planet is either still there or it isn't. He has no way to know. He has no way to find out. The planet's coordinates are in his memory in a format that doesn't map to any navigation system in this part of the galaxy. He can describe the star. He can describe the orbital period. He can describe the way the light hit the east road at noon. None of this information has ever matched a database query.

---

**The ship:**

He was on the Vethari ship for nine weeks. The ship is not designed for prisoners because the Vethari do not take prisoners in any systematic way. Wren was not taken for a purpose. He was taken because a Vethari grabbed him and the neural cascade that constitutes Vethari decision-making did not generate a kill impulse in the 0.3 seconds it takes for the shared field to transmit one. He survived by accident. He kept surviving by being small and by learning, in nine weeks, more about the Vethari than any human institution has assembled in the decades since.

What he learned: they mourn. Between the violence, in the corridors of the ship that are not designed for human proportions, they hold things and they grieve. The sound carries through the hull. It is not language anymore but it was once. A Vethari who is grieving is temporarily less connected — the grief creates a kind of neurological static that dampens the shared field. This is the only window of individual agency the fungus permits, and it is the window Wren used to escape.

The escape: he found a maintenance shaft that opened to an exterior hull access point. The hull access was designed for Vethari maintenance crews who are three times his mass. The bolt mechanism required a tool he didn't have. He spent four days fabricating a lever from a strut he pulled from a damaged interior panel. The Vethari who found him at the hull access was in a grief cycle. It looked at him. It looked at the open hatch. It looked at the fungal filaments along its own forearm — the whitish-reddish threads visible beneath the skin like veins in the wrong color.

The Vethari killed itself. It broke its own cervical structure with its hands. The motion was precise — the ledger-precision that the species once applied to everything. It did this because the shared field would have transmitted its awareness of Wren's position to every connected individual on the ship within seconds. The only way to prevent the transmission was to sever the connection. The only way to sever the connection was to die.

Wren went through the hatch. He was twelve years old. He does not talk about the nine weeks. He does not talk about the Vethari who killed itself. If pressed — and nobody presses, because nobody in this part of the galaxy believes the story and the few who would believe it are the kind of people you don't tell — he says "I was on a ship and then I wasn't." This is the complete truth.

---

**The twelve years after:**

He was picked up by a freight hauler two days after the escape. The hauler's crew spoke a language he didn't know, in a part of space he couldn't identify, running cargo on routes he couldn't map. They dropped him at the first station they docked at, which was a trading post in what Wren would later learn was the outer industrial band of a solar system he'd never heard of.

He was twelve. He didn't speak the language. He had no money, no identification, no legal status, no records in any system. He was a child standing on a dock in a station where the air tasted like metal and the lighting was 60% spectrum and nobody asked where he came from because nobody on that dock had come from anywhere worth asking about.

He lived on stations for twelve years. He learned the language by listening to dock workers argue about cargo weights. He learned to hack by watching maintenance techs leave diagnostic ports open. He learned to read people by sitting in bars where the clientele paid for drinks with scrip and left with information. He was good at cards because cards are pattern recognition and Wren's pattern recognition had been calibrated by nine weeks of tracking Vethari grief cycles to predict windows of safety. A card table is a simpler system.

By twenty he was running with a crew. Five people: Wren (the tech), a pilot named Sable who taught him to fly and who is now dead of something unrelated, a fixer named Caro who is somewhere in the outer sectors and hasn't been seen in three years, a muscle named Dak who was exiled from Concord space for something he won't discuss, and the boss — a man called Torren who ran the crew the way a good card player runs a table: loose, fast, and always one hand ahead of the room.

Torren was killed two years later when a syndicate outfit took over his territory. The takeover was not hostile in the way violence is hostile. It was administrative. The syndicate offered Torren a position. Torren declined. The syndicate sent a man who shot Torren in a bar while Torren was still explaining why the position didn't suit his temperament. The man who shot him ordered a drink afterward and charged it to Torren's tab.

---

**The heist, the artifact, and the betrayal:**

After Torren died, the crew fractured. Wren kept working with one person: a man named Callum, who had joined Torren's operation in its last year and who was the only member of the expanded crew that Wren trusted enough to work with after the boss was gone. Callum was smart in the way that makes you underestimate how ambitious he is. He talked slow and listened fast and never asked for more than his cut. Wren liked him because Callum never asked about the years before the crew. Not once.

The job was a vault extraction at a Concord evidence storage facility. The vault contained seized materials from multiple cases, organized by case number and sealed under Concord administrative protocol. Callum had the layout. Wren had the bypass. The job was clean, fast, and profitable. Among the sealed items in the vault was a crate marked "UNKNOWN COMPOSITE — UNCLASSIFIED" containing a fragment of hull material that weighed approximately 4 kilograms and was filed as evidence in a case that had been closed for eight years with no resolution.

Wren recognized it. The metallurgy was wrong for anything human-manufactured. The surface had a texture he had touched before, in the dark, in a maintenance shaft on a ship that blocked out the sun. And along one edge, baked into the metal by heat and pressure and time, were traces of whitish-reddish filament — dried, dead, but unmistakable if you had spent nine weeks learning to avoid the living version.

The fragment was a piece of Vethari hull. It was the first physical evidence Wren had encountered in ten years that anything he remembered from his childhood had been real. In a galaxy where the Fermi paradox is accepted as settled fact — where humanity has spread across hundreds of systems without encountering another intelligent species — this fragment was proof. Not proof anyone would accept on his word. But proof that could be analyzed, sequenced, tested. Proof that the metallurgy was non-human. Proof that the biological material was from a species not in any database.

He told Callum. This was his mistake.

Callum didn't sell the fragment immediately. He waited three months, during which he and Wren continued to work jobs together and Callum said nothing about the fragment and Wren assumed the fragment was in the stash where they'd put it. It was not. Callum had contacted a buyer through channels that Wren, even with his skills, couldn't trace. The buyer paid. Callum took his share and the fragment and disappeared.

What Callum left behind: Wren's name on the vault job's security footage, anonymized but recoverable. Wren was arrested eleven days later by a Concord sweep team that was not looking for vault robbers. They were looking for an unrelated bounty target and Wren's biometrics tripped a secondary flag in the sweep database. The vault job evidence was in his file before the sweep team finished processing him. He was transferred to a prison transport.

---

**The prison ship:**

The transport was a Concord penal vessel running a route that took sixty days to complete and carried between four hundred and six hundred inmates in a hold designed for two hundred. The ship did not dock during transit. Food was dispensed from automated stations. Medical attention was not available. The guards occupied a sealed section forward of the hold and interacted with the population through cameras, intercoms, and airlock controls.

What happened on the prison ship is what happens when six hundred people are locked in a space designed for two hundred with no supervision and no consequence for sixty days. The fights started on day three over bunk space and by day nine they were over water access and by day fifteen nobody was fighting over anything specific because the fighting had become the system's resting state. The guards watched on cameras. The guards had a schedule for when they vented sections to restore order. The schedule was optimized for camera coverage, not for the people inside the sections.

Wren survived because he was useful: he could fix the water recyclers, he could bypass the food dispenser rationing, he could read the guard rotation from the camera timing. People who are useful in a closed system are protected by the people who benefit from the utility. This is not loyalty. It is the same math that keeps a maintenance tech alive in a war zone. He watched the math work for forty-three days. He watched it fail for others who were not useful, or who were useful to the wrong faction, or who were useful and then stopped being useful when the thing they could fix broke in a way that couldn't be fixed.

On day forty-three, during a fight in the lower hold that killed eleven people and injured thirty, Wren accessed the ship's emergency systems through a maintenance panel that the guards hadn't secured because the panel was in a section of the hold that the cameras didn't cover. He opened an exterior airlock. He went through it in a pressure suit he had assembled from salvaged maintenance equipment over the preceding three weeks. He was in open space for four hours before hypothermia shut down his suit's heating element. He was frozen — not dead, but in a state of metabolic suspension that the suit's medical subsystem induced to preserve neural function — for approximately four years.

He was recovered by a salvage crew that was scanning debris fields for hull material. The crew's medic revived him. The process took three weeks. He lost partial function in his left hand. He did not lose any memory. He remembers all of it.

The salvage crew dropped him at a station. A different station than the one where he'd started as a twelve-year-old, but the same kind of station: outer industrial, bad air, 60% spectrum. A woman who ran cargo out of the station's secondary dock owed a debt to the salvage crew's captain. The debt was settled by giving Wren a ship. The ship was the Tessera. It had a reactor that worked and a hull that held and a transponder ID that had been through enough owners that the registry entry was a palimpsest of names nobody remembered.

Wren was twenty-six. He had been alive for twenty-six years and awake for twenty-two of them and none of those years had been easy and he did not expect the remaining ones to be different.

---

**Six months ago:**

A cargo job through the outer industrial belt. Standard manifest: components, labeled and sealed, origin Meridian Exchange, destination a station Wren had visited before. The seals were Concord. The weight was right. The job was right. The pay was fifteen percent above market rate for the route, which Wren noticed and filed under the part of his brain that tracks tells.

Midway through transit, a scan anomaly flagged one crate. The anomaly was minor — a material density reading that didn't match the manifest description. Wren opened the crate because a density mismatch on a Concord-sealed crate is the kind of thing that gets you arrested at the next gate if the customs officer is having a bad day.

Inside the crate, wrapped in packing foam between two layers of legitimate industrial components, was a sealed container marked "UNCLASSIFIED COMPOSITE — EVIDENCE TRANSFER." The container held a fragment of metallic material, approximately 3 kilograms. Different shape than the one from the vault. Same metallurgy. Same surface texture. Same traces of whitish-reddish biological material along one edge, dried but present.

A second Vethari hull fragment. Filed in a Concord system. Being moved between facilities. The case file reference on the container was alphanumeric and Wren memorized it and sealed the crate and delivered the cargo and said nothing to anyone.

He knew before he opened the crate. Fifteen percent above market on a clean manifest is not a job. It is an invitation. He took it anyway, the way you call a bet you know is a trap because the alternative is folding and never seeing the cards. A pilot who survived a Vethari ship and a prison break does not miss a tell that obvious. He missed nothing. He chose to look.

Someone selected him. The job was placed for him to find. The density mismatch was there for him to notice. The sealed container was packed where a competent tech would look. Wren has no idea who, and this is the part that keeps him awake: whoever selected him has access to sealed Concord evidence, knowledge of a fourteen-year-old vault robbery, and a reason to put a piece of alien hull plating in front of a man who spent nine weeks on a Vethari ship. That is not a random actor. That is someone inside the system, using the system's own logistics to move a piece of evidence that the system is designed to suppress.

The selector's motives are the question Wren cannot answer. But the evidence has a direction. The selector chose a civilian with a personal stake, no institutional loyalty, and a confirmed history with non-human material. The selector routed the fragment through civilian logistics designed to look like a normal cargo job. The selector packed the density mismatch where a competent tech would find it. This is the behavior of someone inside the system who wants evidence to leave the system and can't carry it out themselves — a whistleblower operating through the only channel the bureaucracy can't audit, because the bureaucracy doesn't know the channel exists. A good person doing the right thing through channels that make the right thing look like smuggling.

Wren considers darker readings in the hours he doesn't sleep. The selector might need Wren to find Callum's buyer for reasons that have nothing to do with the fragment and everything to do with what the buyer knows. The selector might be testing whether evidence can be leaked without the system noticing, and Wren is the canary. He considers these readings because considering them is what keeps you alive. But the evidence points one way, and Wren trusts evidence over paranoia because paranoia is free and evidence costs something.

The case file reference traces to a sealed Concord investigation that was opened fourteen years ago and has never been closed. The investigation has no public summary. The investigation's administrative authority is listed as "LOGISTICS OVERSIGHT — REF 44-C."

REF 44-C.

The same reference code that appears on contract authorizations. The same reference code that Hale uses for customs clearance. The same reference code that governs atmospheric allocation budgets. The same code, now attached to a sealed investigation into a fragment of non-human hull material that shouldn't exist in a galaxy where humanity has decided it is alone.

Wren does not know what this means. He knows three things: the fragment is real. Someone is moving it. And the administrative code attached to the investigation is the same code that runs through every corrupt transaction in the sector like a thread through a needle.

---

**What he wants:**

He wants to find Callum. Not for revenge — revenge is expensive and Wren's margins don't support it. He wants to know who Callum sold the first fragment to. Because whoever bought a piece of non-human hull material from a vault thief knows what it is. And whoever knows what it is might know where it came from. And whoever knows where it came from might know the coordinates of a planet that doesn't appear on any chart in this part of the galaxy.

What Wren suspects and what the B5 data confirms: Callum's buyer put the fragment back. The data chip traces to a Concord evidence processing facility. The fragment was "stolen" from evidence storage, sold to a man who paid well and didn't give a name, and the man returned it to evidence storage under the same REF 44-C filing code. The chain of custody is a circle. The system processed Callum's vault robbery as a transfer — one facility to a broker to another facility — and continued filing. The fragment was never outside the system. The system just outsourced its own logistics to a thief who didn't know what he was carrying and a buyer who did.

This is the closed loop that makes the selector's action legible. Someone inside the system watched the first fragment get stolen and returned and filed and sealed, and decided that the second fragment needed to reach someone who would not return it. Someone who would carry it and keep carrying it because the cargo was personal, not institutional. Someone whose relationship to the evidence was not administrative but human. The selector chose Wren because Wren is the one person in the system who would look at a piece of alien hull plating and see a way home instead of a filing problem.

He doesn't say this. He doesn't say "I want to find my sister." He doesn't say "I want to go home." He says "I have a lead on a job that went sideways" and leaves it at that, because in this part of the galaxy a job that went sideways is the only kind of motivation anyone understands without asking follow-up questions.

---

**What he carries:**

The memory of a Vethari killing itself so he could live.

Wren spent nine weeks learning their system. He knows the act was arithmetic, not kindness — the old precision applied one last time. He also knows what that means: if the arithmetic still worked, even for three seconds inside a species-wide neurological catastrophe, then the corruption is not total.

He doesn't call this hope. He calls it a data point. He collects data points. He doesn't know what to do with this one yet.

---

**Voice:**

Comfortable register (bar, card table, friendly dock): talks fast, reads the room, slight smile that's diagnostic not social. "Three kings and a pair of sevens. You had the flush but you held too long. Buy me a drink and I'll tell you where the tell was." "The reactor's fine. It's been 'fine' since I bought the ship. The previous owner defined 'fine' as 'hasn't killed anyone yet,' which is also how I define most of my relationships." "You want to know my rate? My rate is whatever yours is plus the amount you were going to stiff me. Saves us both a conversation." The talking is pattern recognition performed aloud. He's not being charming. He's calibrating.

Working register (contracts, transit, danger): near-silent. Short status updates to nobody. "Gate in four." "Weight's off." "Don't." The silence isn't brooding — it's the same focus he learned in the maintenance shaft on the Vethari ship, where sound meant detection and detection meant the shared field and the shared field meant every Vethari on the ship knew where he was.

Cornered register (interrogation, betrayal, old memories): flat, precise, no affect. "I was on a ship and then I wasn't." "The job went sideways. These things happen." "Callum made a business decision. I would have done the same thing." He would not have done the same thing. He knows this. The sentence is armor, not truth.

What he never does: monologue. Explain himself. Narrate his own feelings. Perform toughness. Reference his backstory unprompted. The past comes out in details — flinching at a specific sound frequency, knowing how Vethari hull metal feels before he touches it, reading a room's air quality by taste before the sensor confirms it.

**The crew (current, at game start):**

Wren works alone and has for five years. He is not antisocial. He is economical. Crew costs money and trust and both are finite. The Tessera is a solo-operable ship.

The game's crew system introduces NPCs who ride with the player. These are not friends. They are people whose utility currently outweighs their cost. Each crew member thinks they're in their own story. They are all in Wren's story. Wren is in theirs. Nobody knows the whole picture.

---

**The artifact:**

The artifact is boring. This is the point.

A fragment of metallic material, dark gray with a greenish tint under certain light spectra, approximately 3 kilograms. Surface texture is smooth but not polished — machined to tolerances that don't match any known manufacturing process. Material analysis returns inconclusive results: the alloy composition includes elements in ratios that don't occur naturally and don't match any industrial standard in any database.

Along one edge, baked into the metal: traces of biological material. Whitish-reddish filaments, dried and inert. To a xenobiologist with the right equipment, this is evidence of a species not catalogued in any database. To everyone else, it's mold on a piece of weird metal.

The artifact has no power. It doesn't glow. It doesn't activate. It doesn't open doors or unlock anything. It is a fragment of hull plating from a Vethari ship that has been through atmospheric reentry, partial melting, recovery, storage, evidence processing, vault storage, theft, sale, resale, and transfer between Concord facilities under administrative codes that connect it to an investigation that has been open for fourteen years and will never close because closing it would require acknowledging what it is.

The artifact matters because of what it proves, not because of what it does. It proves that the Fermi paradox is wrong. It proves that something non-human exists. And whoever has been moving it between facilities under REF 44-C knows this and has known it for fourteen years and has decided that the correct administrative response is to keep moving it.

The physical object is insignificant. The chain of custody is the intelligence.

---

**Design integration — how the protagonist layer connects to the existing worldbuilding:**

The systemic story (Contract 47-A, ATMO DEBT, the 8-NPC ecology, Director Vale) is the world. Wren's personal story (the artifact, Callum, the Vethari, Lida) is the thread that moves through it.

The two stories intersect at REF 44-C. The same administrative code that governs customs clearance, atmospheric allocation, and contract authorization also governs the sealed investigation into non-human material. This is not a conspiracy. It is the same insight as the existing worldbuilding: REF 44-C is a regulatory framework that governs everything because the people who wrote it correctly understood that customs, logistics, atmosphere, governance, and the suppression of evidence that would destabilize the framework are all the same system.

Wren doesn't know about Contract 47-A's real nature. He doesn't know about the recycler theft. He doesn't know about ATMO DEBT or the Slow Gray or the Silt economy. He is running the same contracts as everyone else, getting stiffed the same way, reading the same graffiti. The systemic story happens to him the way it happens to everyone. His personal story — the artifact, the search for Callum, the search for coordinates that might lead to a planet that might still have a grain silo with a crawlspace beneath it — runs underneath, visible only to him.

The game's two stories never merge into one revelation. The player can pursue both. Neither resolves the other. The systemic rot is not caused by the aliens. The aliens are not explained by the systemic rot. They coexist the way real problems coexist: independently, simultaneously, and with no shared solution.

The Vethari are always off-frame. Always present. The fungus is the system. The ledger is the only honest record. The mass is always the same. Only the manifest changes.
