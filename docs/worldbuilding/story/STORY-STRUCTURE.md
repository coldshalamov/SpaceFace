**SPACEFACE — STORY STRUCTURE (DUAL-THREAD)**

Two stories run simultaneously. Neither explains the other. Neither resolves the other. The player is in both.

---

**THREAD A: THE SYSTEM (existing worldbuilding)**

This is the world: Contract 47-A, the recycler theft, the 8-NPC ecology, ATMO DEBT, Director Vale, the Silt economy, the five endgame choices. The systemic story happens to Wren the way it happens to everyone who works the contracts. He runs jobs. He gets stiffed. He reads the graffiti. The HUD lies. The weight never matches. The system is rotting and he is part of the rot because participating is the price of breathing.

Thread A does not require the protagonist to have a backstory. It functions on anyone who runs the contracts long enough. This is its strength and its horror — the system doesn't care who you are. It processes you regardless.

**THREAD B: THE SEARCH (protagonist's personal story)**

This is Wren's story: the artifact, Callum, the Vethari, Lida, the planet that doesn't appear on any chart. Thread B is the personal investigation that runs beneath the professional surface. Wren searches for the artifact's chain of custody while running contracts and getting stiffed and reading the same graffiti as everyone else.

Thread B is invisible to NPCs. Nobody in the sector knows Wren's backstory. Nobody believes in aliens. The few people Wren has told over the years filed it under "spacer who drinks too much and talks about things that didn't happen." The artifact is the only evidence. The artifact is boring. The search is conducted in Wren's margins — between contracts, in secondary logs, through data he pulls from systems while he's supposed to be doing something else.

---

**HOW THE THREADS INTERSECT:**

The threads share one connection: REF 44-C.

The same administrative code governs customs clearance (Hale), contract authorization (Vale), atmospheric allocation (the Silt economy), AND the sealed Concord investigation into non-human material evidence. This is not a conspiracy reveal. It is the same insight the existing worldbuilding already makes: REF 44-C is a regulatory framework written by people who understood that customs, logistics, atmosphere, and the suppression of inconvenient evidence are all the same system. Adding "evidence of alien life" to the list of things REF 44-C manages doesn't change the system. It confirms the system's scope.

The player discovers this intersection gradually. The contract work keeps surfacing REF 44-C in its bureaucratic function. The artifact search keeps surfacing REF 44-C in its suppression function. The two uses of the same code converge not because the stories merge but because the code was always doing both jobs.

---

**THREAD B BEATS (layered onto existing story spine):**

**B0–B1 (early game):** Thread B is present only as texture. On first launch, before the player has done anything, the Tessera's cargo manifest includes one persistent entry at the bottom of the list: "UNCLASSIFIED COMPOSITE — 3.1 KG." No description. No origin. No destination. It cannot be sold, jettisoned, or scanned. It is inventory that the game treats as part of the ship. If the player inspects it, the only additional text is: "PERSONAL EFFECTS." The item sits in the manifest for the entire game. It is the second fragment.

Otherwise, Wren is running contracts. The artifact search is background — he's been working leads for six months and has nothing actionable. The game establishes Thread A: weight discrepancies, graffiti, the HUD's first lies. But the 3.1 kg is always there at the bottom of the manifest, and the weight of the ship is always 3.1 kg heavier than the sum of its listed cargo.

**B2 (First Blood):** After the Elroy kill, Wren accesses the after-action database to file the bounty. While in the system, he runs a query on the case file reference he memorized from the artifact crate. The query returns: SEALED — LOGISTICS OVERSIGHT — REF 44-C. The player sees this in a secondary screen. It is not highlighted. It is not explained. The player can dismiss it and continue.

**B3–B4 (mid game):** Thread B surfaces as optional investigation nodes. At stations where Wren docks for contract work, he can access data terminals and run searches. The searches return fragments: a transfer record here, a facility designation there, a name on an administrative authorization that matches a name on a different administrative authorization. The fragments are boring. They are bureaucratic metadata. They are the chain of custody for a piece of metal that nobody in the sector would recognize as important.

One search returns a name: CALLUM OAKES. Current status: ACTIVE. Last known location: MERIDIAN EXCHANGE. Occupation: INDEPENDENT BROKER. The name sits in the player's log. It doesn't flash. It doesn't trigger a mission. Callum is at Meridian Exchange. Wren can go to Meridian Exchange whenever he wants.

**B5 (Proving Ground):** If the player goes to Meridian Exchange and finds Callum, Callum is not hiding. He's not a villain lurking in a back room. He's a broker with a booth at the exchange, trading in commodity futures, wearing clean clothes. He has a good reputation. He is polite. He does not recognize Wren immediately — it has been seven years and Wren spent four of them frozen.

When Callum recognizes him, the conversation is not dramatic:

"Wren."
"Callum."
"You look different."
"I was frozen for four years."
"I heard something about that."
"Who'd you sell it to?"
"That's not a conversation for the floor."
"I'm not on the floor. I'm at your booth."
"You're at my booth with a question I'm not going to answer where the exchange can hear it."
"The exchange can hear whatever it wants. Who bought the fragment?"
"A man who paid well and didn't give a name. That's the whole story."
"That's not the whole story. That's the part of the story you're willing to tell while the exchange can hear it."
"That's the same thing, Wren."

Callum gives Wren a data chip. The data chip contains a partial transaction record — enough to trace the buyer to a facility, not enough to identify the buyer. The facility is in Concord administrative space. The facility's designation appears in the REF 44-C system under "EVIDENCE PROCESSING — LONG-TERM STORAGE."

Callum does this not out of guilt but because Wren at his booth is bad for business and a data chip is cheaper than a scene. Callum thinks this resolves Wren's problem. It does not resolve Wren's problem. It gives Wren the next link in a chain that Callum never understood because Callum never asked what the fragment was. Callum sold a piece of weird metal for money. The fact that it was proof of alien life was not relevant to the transaction.

**B6 (Empire Seed):** Thread B and Thread A begin running in parallel with increasing proximity. The REF 44-C trail from the artifact leads to the same administrative layer that the ATMO DEBT system runs through. The same facilities that store sealed evidence also process atmospheric allocation appeals. The same personnel who authorized the artifact's transfer between facilities also signed off on the Pit's viability score reduction in year 3.

This is not "the aliens caused the Pit's problems." The Pit's problems were caused by the recycler theft, which was an economic crime by humans for human reasons. The artifact trail runs through the same bureaucracy because the bureaucracy handles everything. The aliens and the Silt and the customs fraud and the atmospheric debt are all filed under the same code because the code was written to file everything.

The player who is tracking both threads sees this convergence as structural, not causal. The system doesn't suppress alien evidence because of the aliens. The system suppresses alien evidence because the system suppresses everything inconvenient, and proof that humanity is not alone in the universe is inconvenient in exactly the same way that proof of atmospheric fraud is inconvenient: it would require acknowledging that the official record is incomplete.

**B7 (The Deep Reach):** At Ashfall Reach, the Kurtz figure's ledger contains an entry that Thread B has been leading toward. A single line in the section marked "ANOMALOUS MATERIALS — UNRESOLVED": a transfer code, a facility designation, and a set of coordinates.

The coordinates are not for a planet in this system. They are for a location that the Kurtz figure logged eleven years ago from an intercepted Concord transmission — a set of numbers in a format Wren does not recognize. Not Concord notation. Not standard galactic. Not the agricultural system his mother used. Something else entirely. A coordinate format that appears in no navigation database Wren can access.

The numbers could be for his home planet. They could be for any of the systems the Vethari ship visited over twelve hundred years. They could be for a research facility. They could be for nothing — a test string, a calibration artifact, a dead entry in a dead database. Wren cannot tell. The format doesn't match anything he knows. He can feel the possibility the way you feel a card you haven't turned over yet: it could be the hand. It could be nothing. You don't know until you have the translation key, and the translation key is not in the ledger.

The game does not resolve this. The coordinates are a cargo item: "NAVIGATIONAL DATA — 1 FILE / 0.01t." The player carries it. The game's endgame choices still apply. None of them include "go home." Going home is not one of the five options because the game is about what you do in the system you're in, not about escaping to a system that may no longer exist.

But the coordinates are there. In the cargo hold. In the data file. After the credits. After the choice. The Tessera's navigation computer has a file it can't process because the format requires a translation key the computer doesn't have. Wren doesn't know if decoding the format would give him home or just another empty system on a twelve-hundred-year-old survey route. The file sits in the ship's memory alongside the transponder history and the manifests and the logs. It weighs 0.01 tonnes. It might weigh everything.

The graffiti in the player's airlock after the endgame, regardless of which choice they made, adds one new line beneath THEY KNEW THE MASS:

*THE COORDINATES DON'T MATCH ANYTHING ON FILE.*

---

**THREAD B IN THE ENDGAME:**

Regardless of which endgame choice the player makes, two cargo items persist: the fragment (UNCLASSIFIED COMPOSITE — 3.1 KG) and, if the player found the coordinates at Ashfall Reach, the navigational data (NAVIGATIONAL DATA — 1 FILE / 0.01t). Neither can be sold. Neither can be jettisoned. The game treats them as PERSONAL EFFECTS.

What Thread B does to each choice:

**Choice A (Clean Uniform):** Wren joins Concord. His record is expunged. The fragment is now in the cargo hold of a Concord Auxiliary vessel. The sealed investigation under REF 44-C is now accessible to him — he has the clearance. He does not access it. The game offers no prompt to access it. The fragment sits in the hold of a clean ship with a clean manifest and the manifest does not list it because personal effects are not listed on Concord manifests. It is still there.

**Choice B (Same Silence):** Wren becomes a routing coordinator. The fragment is in a ship that no longer appears in public records. The navigational data is in a system that no longer associates data with a named operator. The search for Lida continues in the margins of logistics operations that Wren coordinates for other pilots. The margins are wider now. The resources are greater. The question is the same.

**Choice C (Loop-back):** The Tessera returns to Sector 1. The fragment is in the hold. The 3.1 kg is in the manifest, the same line item it has been since the beginning. The coordinates, if found, are also there. Contract 47-A is pending. The search starts over. The mass is the same.

**Choice D (Ledger Continues):** Wren stays at Ashfall Reach. The Kurtz figure's ledger and the fragment sit in the same room. Two records that the system was designed to suppress, kept by a person the system has classified as deceased. The coordinates, if found, are filed in the ledger alongside twenty years of cargo fraud. The same columns. The same handwriting. The planet that might hold Lida is one line in a ledger of a thousand lines, each one equally unresolved.

**Choice E (Next Run):** The fragment is in the hold. Contract 47-B is pending. The coordinates are in the navigation computer. Wren accepts the next job. The search continues between contracts, in the margins, the way it always has. Nothing changes. Everything continues. The mass of 3.1 kg and 0.01t are carried forward into whatever comes next, alongside reactor fuel and hull patches and the transponder history of a ship that has had too many owners to remember.

---

**WHAT THREAD B IS NOT:**

Thread B is not the "real" story that Thread A was hiding. Thread A is the story of the world. Thread B is the story of one person in it. The world does not care about Wren's backstory. The world processes him through the same contracts and the same graffiti and the same HUD lies as everyone else. His personal tragedy does not exempt him from the systemic rot. His search for his sister does not make him noble. It makes him a person with a private reason to run the same jobs everyone runs for money.

The Vethari are not the secret behind the system. The system's secret is that it has no secret — it operates exactly as designed. The Vethari are a separate horror that exists independently, off-frame: always present, never the immediate problem, the thing that makes the smaller problems feel both more urgent and more futile.

Thread B gives every contract a double weight: the weight the manifest lists and the weight Wren carries from a planet nobody's heard of. The two weights never reconcile. The mass is always different depending on who's counting.

---

**DOES THE STORY EARN ITS VIOLENCE?**

The Vethari violence is not action-movie violence. It is the violence of a system failure — a neurological architecture collapsing under the weight of shared perception. The Vethari don't choose to destroy planets. They can't choose not to. The fungus doesn't choose to corrupt them. The fungus is an organism doing what organisms do. The result is catastrophic and nobody is responsible and the only moral act available to an individual Vethari is self-destruction, which is both the most precise act of the old ledger-system and the most complete repudiation of it.

Violence with grammar before it has cause. The Vethari grammar is the ledger. The cause is the fungus. The grammar predates the cause by millennia. The violence is the grammar operating without the moral framework that once constrained it.

The human violence — the contracts, the kills, the systemic rot — is the same structure at a smaller scale. The grammar is the administrative code. The cause is the economic incentive. REF 44-C predates every corrupt transaction it governs. The system's grammar produces the violence. The violence is correct, by the grammar, every time.

---

**DOES THE STORY EARN ITS SADNESS?**

The sadness is not about losing. It's about winning at a game that costs more than winning is worth and playing it anyway because the alternative is admitting the game was never worth the cost. The search for coordinates that may lead to a planet that may no longer exist, containing a grain silo that may have a crawlspace that may still hold a girl who would now be twenty-eight and who has been alone for nineteen years if she's alive at all.

The sadness earns its place because the story never confirms or denies the outcome. Lida is not rescued. Lida is not confirmed dead. The game refuses to answer because answering would end the weight, and the weight is the point.

The coordinates exist. The player carries them. The game ends. The question remains open. The mass of the question is 0.01 tonnes and the manifest calls it NAVIGATIONAL DATA and the ship carries it alongside everything else.
