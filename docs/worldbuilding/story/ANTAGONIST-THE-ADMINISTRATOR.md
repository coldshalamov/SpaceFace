**SPACEFACE — THE ANTAGONIST: DIRECTOR VALE**

The story spine has eight beats and no face on the system. This is the face.

---

**Who they are:**

Director Vale. No first name in any public record. Concord Auxiliary, Mid-Sector Administrative Division, Logistics Oversight. The title is real. The division is real. What the division actually does is not listed anywhere.

Vale is the person who posts Contract 47-A. Not personally — they don't post contracts. But the chain from Vale's signature to the job that withheld the player's payment in the opening run is exactly three links long, and any player who runs enough contracts and pays attention to the paperwork will eventually be able to count them.

Vale is not a villain. This is important. Vale is a practical person who understands what the system is and has concluded that understanding what the system is requires participating in it, which requires not thinking too hard about what participation means. This is Le Carré's gray man: not evil, not corrupt in any theatrical sense. Just the person who signs the order that gets your friends spaced, and who then goes home to a warm room and eats food that doesn't taste like an apology.

---

**How Vale appears:**

**B1 ("Honest Work"):** Vale's name appears on a contract. Not on the board — on the authorization line of the contract document, which is visible if the player reads the full contract text (most don't). The authorization reads "APPROVED: VALE, D. / MID-SECTOR ADMIN / REF 44-C." Nothing else.

**B3 ("Bigger Boat"):** A comms popup from an unnamed sender: "Your recent work has been noted. The board will have something appropriate for your current capacity." No signature. The message header, if the player checks it, shows origin: Concord Relay 3, forwarded through four intermediate stations. One of the forwarding stations is the one where Hale works customs. The message arrives the cycle after the player completes their first B2 bounty.

**B4 ("Pick a Side"):** The faction intro contracts available at B4 — Traders Guild, Patrol Authority, Free Captains — are all routed through the same clearing station. The clearing station's administrator is listed in the station records as "V. Director, acting." Not an alias. Just how it's filed.

**B5 ("Proving Ground"):** The mission chain includes one job where the target is a ship that, upon investigation, turns out to be a former Concord vessel with an expired registry. The ship's last registered owner, in the database, is "VALE HOLDINGS LLC." The mission brief doesn't mention this. The after-action report doesn't mention it either. The ship's cargo manifest, accessible only after the salvage mission, shows it was carrying documentation: "ADMINISTRATIVE RECORDS — 3 YEARS / SEALED."

**B6 ("Empire Seed"):** A message arrives with a specific contract number attached: "Contract 47-A has been re-opened for settlement. Payment pending. Please advise availability." The contract number is the one from the opening run. The payment amount is correct. The message sender is "D. VALE / ADMIN / PRIORITY." The player can accept the settlement or not. The settlement, if accepted, pays the original amount — and adds the player to a roster that later appears in the Ashfall Reach ledger as a named asset.

**Ashfall Reach / B7:** The Kurtz figure's ledger includes a section on "Administrative Counterparties." Vale's name appears in the column labeled "BENEFICIARY." Not as a villain. As a line item. The beneficiary of the system's outputs. The ledger is factual. It doesn't editorialize. Vale is just the name at the end of the money trail.

---

**What Vale says (one line, ever):**

If the player is at Trusted or higher with Concord and reaches the clearing station during B5, a comms popup fires: "Good work. Keep it clean." That's all. No face. No meeting. Just the line.

The line is exactly what the wardens used to say at The Pit when you'd completed your shift quota without incident. The player may or may not notice this. The game doesn't point it out.

---

**Why Vale is not a villain:**

Villains are satisfying. They can be confronted, exposed, destroyed. A villain's defeat means the system had a bad actor and removing the bad actor fixes the system.

Vale is not a bad actor. Vale is a good administrator. The contracts are filed correctly. The authorizations are legal. The payments, when they arrive, are accurate. The fact that the system produces outcomes like Contract 47-A is not Vale's fault. Vale didn't design the system. Vale just administers it.

This is the Le Carré insight: the horror is not that bad people run the system. The horror is that the system runs fine with good people running it, and it still produces the same outcomes.

The player cannot expose Vale. There's nothing to expose. The paperwork is clean. The only record that connects Vale to anything the player would recognize as wrong is the Kurtz figure's handwritten ledger, which is not admissible anywhere because nothing about the Ashfall Reach encounter was admissible anywhere.

---

**What happens to Vale:**

Nothing. Vale continues to administer. New contracts appear. The roster grows. The system continues to produce outcomes.

In Choice A (the Clean Uniform ending), the player signs their first document as Concord Auxiliary. The document's approval chain shows their name above Vale's. They have been inserted into the chain between Vale and the next link. The paperwork is correct. The system runs fine.

In Choice B (the Same Silence ending), Vale's contracts stop appearing for the player. Not because of anything that happened. Because the player has become a routing problem and routing problems don't receive direct comms from Administrative Oversight. They receive indirect comms, through four stations, the way everyone does eventually.

In Choice C (the Only Honest Option), Vale is not mentioned. Nothing about the ending involves Vale. This is also the point. The ending that opts out of the system is the only one that doesn't require a relationship with the system's administrator.

---

**Design note:**

Vale should never be given a face, a gender pronoun, or a physical description. The name is a surname. "D." is an initial that doesn't resolve. Administrative titles and reference numbers only. This is not coyness — it is accuracy. The system's administrator is not a person you could identify in a lineup. They're a signature on a document you had to look very hard to find.
