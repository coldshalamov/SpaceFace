**SPACEFACE — SPACER SUPERSTITIONS (EXTENDED CANON)**

The culture is pure spacer superstition dressed up as operational security. This is not metaphor. The superstitions ARE operational security, accumulated from incidents that nobody filed a report on because the people who would have filed the report were the ones who didn't come back.

Every superstition on this list has an origin story that nobody tells out loud. The origin is in the graffiti if you look long enough.

---

**SHIPS:**

Never name a ship after a living person. If the ship is destroyed, the name survives the person or the person survives the ship. Either way, something is haunted.

Never repaint a ship the same color it was when you bought it. The color belongs to the previous owner's record. The scan logs index ships by color signature. Repaint means a new color, not the same color with a second coat.

If the reactor makes a new sound that wasn't there before, log it. Don't investigate it. Log it. The Concord logging system requires incident reports within 48 hours. If you haven't logged it, you didn't hear it. If you didn't hear it, you weren't there when it happened. *(This superstition is about Hale. Nobody says it's about Hale.)*

Never leave a ship docked longer than 72 hours at a station where you owe money. 72 hours is when dock authority reviews outstanding accounts. After 72 hours, the review includes the option to hold the vessel. Nobody knows how Kessler gets the dock authority list but everyone knows Kessler has it.

---

**CONTRACTS:**

Never accept a contract with an even-number reference code. Odd numbers are generated by the public board algorithm. Even numbers are assigned manually. Manual assignments mean someone wanted this job to go to someone specific. That someone is not necessarily you.

Never accept a contract for a destination you've never been to that pays better than a destination you have been to of equivalent distance. The premium exists because something happened at the destination that suppressed the pool of pilots willing to go there.

If the contract says "routine," read the addendum. *(This is specifically about Vael contracts but applies universally.)*

If the contract board doesn't list a faction ID, the faction doesn't want to be listed. This is not the same as the faction not existing. *(This one is about The Quiet.)*

---

**JUMP AND TRANSIT:**

Never jump on a Tuesday. *(This originated from a statistical anomaly in a three-year period when Concord conducted unannounced sweep operations on Tuesdays. The sweeps stopped. The superstition didn't.)*

If the comms glitch three times in one shift, someone's about to die. *(Three glitches means the comms relay is being jammed. Jamming means someone in sensor range doesn't want outgoing traffic. Someone who doesn't want outgoing traffic is planning something that creates incoming damage.)*

Never charge the jump drive in a sector where the gate is behind you. If interdiction hits, you arrive in the ambush pocket facing the wrong direction and the first three seconds are dead time. Three seconds is enough. *(Practical, not superstitious. Still said like a superstition because the people who learned it the hard way are the ones who made it a saying.)*

Never jump through a gate that's being used by a Concord patrol. Wait for the next cycle. The Concord gate log time-stamps jumps in sequence. Your jump is logged immediately after theirs. You're now associated with a Concord patrol jump in the sector record. This is either useful or catastrophic depending on what the Concord patrol was doing, which you won't know until later, which is the problem.

---

**STATIONS AND DOCKING:**

Never use airlock 3. *(Specific to various stations. The reason varies. The number doesn't.)*

If maintenance is working in the corridor outside your berth when you arrive, find another berth. Maintenance at berths means either the previous occupant didn't pay or the previous occupant left something behind that required cleaning. Either way, you're at a berth with a recent history, and recent history is how you end up in someone else's record.

Never eat at a station commissary on the first cycle after a faction ownership change. The transition period is when supply contracts are renegotiated, which is when the food is whatever the lowest bidder had in inventory, which is when the filters haven't been checked, which is when the protein paste ratio goes wrong.

Always leave one credit in a Syndicate account. Zero-balance triggers a review. The review includes a cross-reference of all transactions in the account history. The cross-reference flags anything that matches a pattern. The patterns are defined by the Syndicate. The Syndicate defines them based on what they're looking for. They're always looking for something. *(This superstition is specifically about the MTS.)*

---

**THE GRAFFITI:**

If you see graffiti that mentions your callsign, don't paint over it. You can leave. You shouldn't be able to see it and come back and have it still be there. If it's still there when you come back, you're now the person who left it there in the record.

If you see graffiti that mentions something you haven't done yet, file a flight plan for somewhere else. *(This is the superstition the graffiti writers intended. It's the one warning that isn't dressed up as anything.)*

Never write graffiti in your own airlock. The airlock is the threshold. What you write there is what you're carrying. You'll be carrying it for the next run, and the run after that, and the run after that. *(This superstition is violated at the end of Phase 3 when the player finds "THEY KNEW THE MASS" written in their own hand. Nobody explained the superstition. The player finds out why it existed.)*

---

**THE PIT (surviving superstitions from the colony):**

Never speak a dead warden's name inside a Concord beacon radius. The log will attribute any nearby incident to that name and close the file. *(Operational: the Concord logging system has an auto-attribution protocol for incidents in sectors with no live personnel registered. A dead warden's ID, still active in the system, becomes the responsible party. The file closes. Nobody investigates a closed file.)*

Never ask what the Pit smells like to someone who hasn't been there. They'll describe something wrong and you'll spend the rest of the conversation knowing that what they think they know is off by exactly the amount that matters.

Count your crew before jump. Count them after. If the numbers are different, don't enter a gate until you know why. *(The warden's count at shift change. Carried forward. The reason nobody says why is that everyone who needs to know already does.)*
