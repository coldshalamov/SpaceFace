**VIBE DOCUMENT 03 — STATIC BETWEEN THE STARS**

You fly with one eye on the radar and the other on the truth, except the truth keeps shorting out. The HUD stutters—range numbers flip to question marks, then to "???" that lingers like it's judging you. A targeting reticle blooms red across a hauler that's already venting atmosphere; the IFF tag tries to load, fails, and settles on "UNKNOWN / PROBABLY FUCKED." You fire anyway. The kill feed prints in clean white: +1 Bounty. Then, beneath it, smaller, almost apologetic: *They were carrying medicine.* The text glitches, rewrites itself to *Contraband detected*, then dies. You laugh once, short and ugly, because that’s what you do when the universe lies to your face and you lie right back.

Graffiti is the only honest narrator left. Every station bulkhead you dock at carries fresh paint from crews who won't live long enough to see it fade. *"This sector eats captains twice as mean as you."* *"Mal called—he wants his spine back."* *"The last honest trader hanged himself in airlock 3."* You read them while the airlock cycles and the station broker lowballs your cargo by thirty percent because your rep ticked down again. You could shoot him. You don't. Not today. The HUD records the transaction in green, then adds a jittery footnote only you can see: *You're running out of people who'll still talk to you.* You toggle it off. The footnote stays.

Malcolm Reynolds never existed, but you wear his ghost anyway—duster replaced by scorched pressure suit, six-shooter swapped for a railgun that glows when it overheats. You talk fast when you need to, shoot faster when talking fails, and the game never gives you the luxury of a cutscene to explain why both feel the same now. Every run you tell yourself this haul is clean. Every run the cargo manifest updates with one extra line item that wasn't there at pickup: *Human remains (unclaimed).* The station authority doesn't ask. You don't offer. The dark laugh comes anyway, low and private, because the only difference between a pirate and a trader is who writes the after-action report.

Moral descent doesn't arrive with violins. It arrives in the cargo hold after you vent three boarders who were only trying to steal the same ore you just stole from someone else. The bodies tumble, freeze, and the HUD helpfully tags them *Threat neutralized* before a second line flickers in: *They had families.* You close the log. The next time you check, that second line is gone and a new piece of graffiti has appeared on your own bulkhead, painted in your own hand while you slept: *They all do.* You don't remember writing it. You don't paint over it either.

Dark humor is the pressure valve. When the reactor redlines and the ship AI—voice like a bored hangman—says "Power failure in three… two… probably," you answer out loud, "Story of my life, sweetheart." When a bounty hunter hails you mid-fight with the classic "Stand down or be destroyed," your reply is automatic: "Buddy, I've been destroyed since Tuesday. You're late to the wake." The HUD flashes a rare, clean message across the top of the screen, no glitch this time: *Enemy morale lowered.* You grin through blood in your teeth and keep firing.

The game never shows you becoming the villain. It simply stops correcting the HUD when it lies in your favor. One day the graffiti on your airlock reads *Welcome home, Captain.* You don't know whose hand wrote it. You don't ask. You just dock, sell the cargo, and watch the credits tick up while the kill counter quietly adds one more name you'll forget by the next jump. The static between the stars sounds exactly like laughter if you listen long enough.
